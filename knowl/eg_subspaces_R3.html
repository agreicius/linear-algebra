Example 3.7.15. Subspaces of \(\R^3\).

We describe all subspaces \(W\) of \(\R^3\) ordered by dimension.
  • \(\dim W=0\).
    If \(\dim W=0\text{,}\) then \(W\) must have the empty set \(\{\}\) as a basis (by definition of dimension), and hence \(W=\Span\{ \}=\{\boldzero\}\text{,}\) the zero subspace.
  • \(\dim W=1\).
    If \(\dim W=1\text{,}\) then \(W\) has a basis \(B=\{\boldv\}\) consisting of a single nonzero vector \(\boldv\in \R^3\text{.}\) In this case we have \(W=\Span\{\boldv\}=\{t\boldv\colon t\in \R\}\text{,}\) which is a line passing through the origin with direction vector \(\boldv\text{.}\) Thus \(1\)-dimensional subspaces of \(\R^3\) are lines passing through the origin.
  • \(\dim W=2\).
    If \(\dim W=2\text{,}\) then \(W\) has a basis \(B=\{\boldv_1, \boldv_2\}\) containing two non-parallel vectors in \(\R^3\text{.}\) You learn in multivariable calculus that
    \begin{equation*} W=\span\{\boldv_1, \boldv_2\}=\{s\boldv_1+t\boldv_2\colon s,t\in \R\} \end{equation*}
    is the plane in \(\R^3\) passing through the origin with “direction vectors” \(\boldv_1, \boldv_2\text{.}\) In fact, you can show that the cross product \(\boldn=\boldv_1\times \boldv_2\) is a normal vector for the plane \(W\) in this case. Thus the \(2\)-dimensional subspaces of \(\R^3\) are planes passing through the origin.
  • \(\dim W=3\).
    Assume \(\dim W=3\text{.}\) Since \(W\) is a subspace of \(\R^3\text{,}\) and since \(\dim W=\dim \R^3=3\text{,}\) we must have \(W=\R^3\) according to Corollary 3.7.13. It is as simple as that! Thus \(\R^3\) is the only \(3\)-dimensional subspace of \(\R^3\text{.}\)
