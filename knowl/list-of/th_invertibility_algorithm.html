Proof.

From the proof of TheoremÂ 2.4.5 we know \(A\) is invertible if and only if \(U\) has \(n\) leading ones. The question remains as to why reducing the augmented matrix \(\begin{amatrix}[c|c]A\amp I\end{amatrix}\) to \(\begin{amatrix}[c|c]I\amp C\end{amatrix}\) tells us that \(A^{-1}=C\text{.}\) Let \(E_1, E_2, \dots, E_r\) be the elementary matrices representing the row operations involved in this process. Then we have
\begin{equation*} E_rE_{r-1}\cdots E_1A=I_n\text{.} \end{equation*}
After a little algebra, we see that
\begin{equation*} A^{-1}=E_rE_{r-1}\cdots E_1\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since \(C\) is the result of applying same row operations to \(I_n\) we have
\begin{equation*} C=E_rE_{r-1}\cdots E_1I=E_rE_{r-1}\cdots E_1=A^{-1}\text{,} \end{equation*}
as claimed.
