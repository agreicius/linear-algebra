Proof.

We prove only the first statement. First observe that if \(A\) is \(m\times n\text{,}\) then so is \(B\) and \(A+B\text{.}\) Then \((A+B)^T\) is \(n\times m\) by DefinitionÂ 2.1.29. Similarly, we see that \(A^T+B^T\) is \(n\times m\text{.}\)

Next, given any \((i,j)\) with \(1\leq i\leq n\text{,}\) \(1\leq j\leq m\text{,}\) we have
\begin{align*} \left((A+B)^T\right)_{ij}\amp =(A+B)_{ji} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/d_transpose.html}{\text{Definition 2.1.29}})\\ \amp = A_{ji}+B_{ji} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/d_matrix_add_subtract.html}{\text{Definition 2.1.9}})\\ \amp =(A^T)_{ij}+(B^T)_{ij} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/d_transpose.html}{\text{Definition 2.1.29}} \\ \amp =(A^T+B^T)_{ij} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/d_matrix_add_subtract.html}{\text{Definition 2.1.9}})\text{.} \end{align*}
Since the \(ij\)-entries of both matrices are equal for each \((i,j)\text{,}\) it follows that \((A+B)^T=A^T+B^T\text{.}\)
