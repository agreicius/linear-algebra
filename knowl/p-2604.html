If \(\dim W=2\text{,}\) then \(W\) has a basis \(B=\{\boldv_1, \boldv_2\}\) containing two non-parallel vectors in \(\R^3\text{.}\) You learn in multivariable calculus that
\begin{equation*} W=\Span\{\boldv_1, \boldv_2\}=\{s\boldv_1+t\boldv_2\colon s,t\in \R\} \end{equation*}
is the plane in \(\R^3\) passing through the origin with “direction vectors” \(\boldv_1, \boldv_2\text{.}\) In fact, you can show that the cross product \(\boldn=\boldv_1\times \boldv_2\) is a normal vector for the plane \(W\) in this case. Thus the \(2\)-dimensional subspaces of \(\R^3\) are planes passing through the origin.
